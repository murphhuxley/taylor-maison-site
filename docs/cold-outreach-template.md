## STRATEGY NOTES BEFORE YOU READ A WORD

**Don't pitch. Open a conversation.**

The Hamptons market runs on relationships and warm introductions. Cold email works when it doesn't read like cold email. Every template below is designed to feel like a message from a smart person who noticed something — not a sales pitch from an agency chasing work.

**Two rules:**
1. Always be specific. Name the business. Notice something real about them. Generic = deleted.
2. Earn the second message, not the contract. The goal of the first email is a reply, not a signed SOW.

**Subject line principle:** The best subject lines sound like something a curious person would say, not a marketer.

## TEMPLATE A — The "You're Invisible" Open
*Best for: Facebook-only businesses, no website, clearly leaving money on the table*

---

**Subject:** [Business Name] came up in a search — had to reach out

Hi [Name / Owner name if known],

I was doing some research on [specific category, e.g. "Hamptons wedding florists"] and [Business Name] kept coming up in local conversations — but not in Google results.

I'm Taylor. I run a small creative studio based in Southampton. I build brand identities and websites for Hamptons businesses that want to compete for clients who actually search online. 

I'd love to show you what that could look like for [Business Name]. Fifteen minutes, no pitch deck, just a conversation. 

If you're open to it — [email] / [phone]

Taylor Maison
Taylor Maison Creative
Southampton, NY

---

**Why this works:** It opens with something real (they don't show up in search), it's specific, it's short, and it doesn't ask for a commitment — just a conversation.

## TEMPLATE B — The Portfolio-First Open
*Best for: Businesses that have a bad website (not NO website). Implies they have taste and you noticed.*

---

**Subject:** Quick thought on [Business Name]

Hi [Name],

I've been a fan of [Business Name] for a while. The [specific thing — e.g., "wine program you've built," "the garden design work," "the way you've positioned the space"] is genuinely impressive.

I was on your website recently and — honestly — it doesn't do the work justice. [Specific observation, e.g., "The photography is beautiful but buried in a template that undercuts it."]

I'm Taylor. I do brand and web design work specifically for East End businesses. My work is at [URL]. I grew up in Southampton — so I understand this market from the inside.

I'm not pitching you. Just thought it was worth saying: you've built something great, and the front door should match it.

If you're ever curious what a proper redesign could look like, I'd love to show you.

Taylor Maison
taylormaison.com / [email]

---

**Why this works:** It leads with a genuine compliment that's specific enough to feel real, names the gap without being brutal, and closes softly. The "I'm not pitching you" line paradoxically makes it more persuasive.

## TEMPLATE C — The Seasonal Urgency Play
*Best for: Spring outreach (March–April) when businesses are gearing up for summer season*

---

**Subject:** Before Memorial Day

Hi [Name],

You've got maybe 8 weeks before the season starts.

That's enough time to have a website that actually reflects what [Business Name] is — if we start now.

I'm Taylor Maison. I build custom websites for Hamptons businesses. Recent work: [link to 1–2 relevant portfolio pieces]. I'm based in Southampton starting in May.

If you've been thinking about it, now is literally the right moment.

15 minutes to see if it makes sense?

Taylor
[email] / [phone]

---

**Why this works:** Short. Time-bound. Doesn't oversell. The brevity signals respect for their time (they're busy operators getting ready for season).

## TEMPLATE D — The Warm Network Intro
*Best for: When you have any connection whatsoever — mutual contact, you've been to the restaurant, you met them at an event*

---

**Subject:** [Mutual contact] mentioned I should reach out / Great meeting you at [event]

Hi [Name],

[Specific real reference: "We met briefly at [event]" / "I've been coming to [restaurant] for years" / "[Name] at [company] suggested I reach out."]

I'm working on something I think could be relevant for [Business Name].

I do brand and web design for Hamptons businesses — specifically the ones that have outgrown their current digital presence. My portfolio is at [URL]. I just finished work for [relevant project, e.g., a winery / security firm / event company] and I'm looking for the right next project.

Would love to buy you a coffee and show you what I'm thinking for [Business Name]. No commitment — just a conversation.

Taylor Maison
[email] / [phone]

---

**Why this works:** The mutual connection or genuine personal history converts cold to warm immediately. Even "I've been coming to your restaurant" matters in a small community.

## TEMPLATE E — The Follow-Up (No Response to First Email)
*Send 5–7 days after initial outreach if no response*

---

**Subject:** Re: [original subject]

Hi [Name],

Just following up in case my last message got buried.

One question: when was the last time a potential client found you through your website?

If you don't know — that might be the answer.

Happy to chat whenever the timing is right.

Taylor
[email]

---

**Why this works:** It's short, not pushy, and asks a question they probably don't know the answer to. Curiosity is a better trigger than urgency.

## IN-PERSON APPROACH (For May–September Season)

When you're physically in Southampton, the best outreach isn't email — it's showing up.

**The play:**
1. Walk into the target business as a regular customer first. Know the product/service.
2. On a second visit, introduce yourself to the owner/manager naturally.
3. The line: *"I do brand and web work for businesses on the East End. I've always liked what you're doing here — would you ever be open to a conversation about the digital side?"*
4. If yes: hand them a card, suggest coffee the following week.

**What to carry:** A simple one-page leave-behind with your URL and 2–3 project screenshots. Not a brochure. A conversation piece.

Research swing · Four custom-letterform answers

Round 5 · 2026-04-18 · post-research

The research surfaced four directives: custom letterform work beats stock type (Chanel, Fendi, YSL); if you keep a monogram, the letters must mechanically interact (not two letters in a frame); kill uppercase TM standalone (™ read); the dominant peer pattern is wordmark, not seal. These four concepts respond directly — each is a distinct archetype that would hold up under a Sagi Haviv or Michael Bierut critique, in a way the pearl+stock-letter approach would not. Pick the direction, then we pressure-test against the eight-question framework.

TAYLORMAISON

The move: T and M share a vertical stem — the T's single stem literally is the M's left stem. One continuous glyph. A full horizontal top serves as the T's crossbar and the M's cap. The middle of the glyph has an inverted-V that reads as the M's interior.
Why it works: this is the Chanel / Fendi / VW move — a proprietary interlock you couldn't type in any stock font. Escapes the ™ trap because it reads as one glyph, not two letters + symbol. The shared stem is the signature mechanic.

TAYLORMAISON

The move: just M. Heavy, architectural, with a deep V that meets near the midline. Drawn proprietary (not Cabinet M) — sharper proportions, thicker stems, dramatic interior.
Why it works: "Taylor" is the common half of the name; "Maison" is the distinctive half. The M is the differentiating letter. Maison = house / atelier — a creative director's studio. Escapes the ™ confusion entirely. Reduces the problem from "two letters in a frame" to "one letter with presence."

The move: the wordmark is the mark. No seal, no glyph. But the period after MAISON — a filled pearl-like dot — becomes the signature punctuation. The period is the pearl; the pearl is the period. For favicons/avatars, use just the dot.
Why it works: matches the dominant peer archetype (~12 of 25 peers use pure wordmarks). Sidesteps every monogram pitfall. The period keeps the pearl in the system as a secondary motif — used as bullet points, section breaks, drop-caps — without asking it to carry the business alone. Scher-aligned: type-led, decisive.

The move: take your favorite sunset pearl (Classic — top solid, bottom slatted) and reduce ruthlessly. Solid upper semicircle + one horizontal rule extending edge-to-edge. Two elements total. Max reduction.
Why it works: passes every distinctiveness test — phone-describable ("dome over a line"), memorable, silhouette-proof at 16px, one-color-stable. Keeps the semantic content of the sunset direction (sun / horizon / architectural threshold) without asking viewers to parse slats. Rand would approve: simplicity as byproduct.

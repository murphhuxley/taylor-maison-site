Concept · Pearl as O

The pearl hides inside your name.

Replace the O in TAYLOR with the pearl and the two halves of your identity fuse. The mark stops being an attached signature and becomes a letter — the most distinctive letter in your name. Below: four treatments of the same idea, each tuned to a different tension between integration and legibility.

The argument

The pearl replaces the O as a solid disc. Maximum integration — the signature motif is now load-bearing inside the name, not hanging off it. At small sizes the disc reads as an O because the brain fills in the counter by context. At hero scale the unfilled disc makes the name look almost branded, like a stamp.

Risk: at very small sizes (favicon / 16px) the solid dot can bleed into the adjacent L and R. Tracking may need a touch more air.

The argument

Keep the counter open — the pearl becomes a ring, geometrically perfect, read as both an O and a coin. Legibility is unchallenged at every size. The intervention is quieter; the name reads first and the circle idea lingers.

Risk: quieter = less distinctive. An almost-perfect O in a sans wordmark is a move plenty of brands make; this version trades ownership for safety.

The argument

The Classic sunset pearl sits inside the wordmark. Your favorite motif — solid upper hemisphere, three cuts below — now lives as a letter. The wordmark is the logo; the lockup problem disappears.

Risk: the slats are too fine under 24px; the O goes muddy and reads as a dirty circle. Useable at hero scale and on social, but a simplified solid or ring is needed for small sizes. (Good news: the simplification IS the other two cards.)

Research: §2 letter interaction · §4 master mark as name

The argument

The original pearl — big disc, small knockout up-and-left — now sitting inside your name. The offset inner circle reads as a pearl's highlight, a moon's shadow, or just a charming counter. It's the only variant where the O keeps a real interior shape (not just ring or disc), which makes it the most O-like and also the most distinctive.

At small sizes the knockout can close up and revert to the solid pearl (Card 01) — which is again a decent failure mode. At hero scale the highlight turns the wordmark into something that looks almost jeweled.

Research: §2 counter as signature · §4 original mark preserved · §3 most O-like of the five

The argument

An open ring with a single pupil-dot at the center. Reads as an O with a quirk — a bindi, a bullseye, a pearl on a string. Subtle enough that the name leads; distinctive enough that you have a mark to pull out (the dot becomes your favicon).

Risk: at small sizes the center dot can disappear, reverting this to the ring treatment (Card 02). That's actually not a bad failure mode.

Research: §2 counter as signature · §3 distinctive at scale

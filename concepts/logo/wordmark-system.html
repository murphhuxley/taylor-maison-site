01

Which typographic voice is yours?

Same name, three weights + trackings in Cabinet Grotesk. Each one aligns to one of your references. Pick the register that feels like the business you actually want to run.

The argument

Heavy, confident, tight. Reads as a presence — the name sits like a stamp. This is the voice of A24, Aesop (in its wordmark moods), Everlane's earlier identity. It's the most assertive of the three; not loud, but undeniable in the room.

Fit check: pairs well with the cream + charcoal palette you've got. Risk: can feel slightly heavy-handed if the rest of the site is restrained — the wordmark will want air around it.

The argument

Middle path. Confident but not shouting. Reads as editorial / considered — the voice of an agency that makes careful choices. Pairs with serif body copy or equally-weighted sans; flexible across tones. This is probably the safest lane of the three, which is both its strength and its weakness.

Fit check: least visually specific. If every other touchpoint on the site is doing work, this wordmark will hold its own but won't lead.

The argument

Thin, wide, stately. Reads as signage — the voice of quiet luxury. James Perse, Calvin Klein's wordmark, Loewe's earlier marks. The widest tracking feels like a storefront; it demands breathing room and rewards it.

Fit check: strongest at hero scale, hardest at small sizes. Tracked-out caps can break down under 14px — needs careful kerning and probably a small-size alt (tighter tracking) for UI chrome. It's the most committed-to-a-register of the three; if this is you, it's really you.

02

Does the pearl survive as a system element?

The pearl is not in the logo lockup — that's settled. But it can still live in your brand world. Left: the pearl survives as a secondary graphic. Right: it retires entirely, and the favicon becomes a typographic solution. Both are valid — pick the one that feels right.

YES · Pearl as system element

Wordmark is the logo. Pearl is the signature — appears in favicon, avatar, case study corners, section dividers, email signatures.

Why keep it

Protects your attachment to the pearl without forcing it into the logo. The mark lives in every off-wordmark context: the favicon is the pearl, the Instagram avatar is the pearl, case studies get a subtle pearl stamp in the corner, section dividers use a small pearl as a bullet. Present everywhere, never the logo itself.

Risk: every system element is one more thing to maintain and defend. If the pearl doesn't actually mean anything durable to the work, it's decoration.

NO · Pure wordmark system

Pearl retires entirely. Favicon and avatar become typographic. The wordmark, at different sizes, is the only mark you have. Maximum A24.

Why retire it

One asset. One idea. The wordmark, at every scale, is the system. Favicon is a single "T" (or "tm" lowercase to dodge ™ confusion). Case study corners use the wordmark small. Email signature is the wordmark and nothing else. This is Rand at full commitment: the name does all the work.

Risk: you lose a beloved shape. If the pearl actually carries meaning for you that the wordmark doesn't, retiring it is a real cost, not a simplification.

Drawn from your own initials in Cabinet Grotesk 800 — same typeface as the wordmark, so the anchor and the voice come from the same architecture. Four different locks: stacked totem, crossed ligature, anchored signature, punctuated initials. All four are proprietary the moment they're rendered in your typeface. None exist anywhere else. Pick the one that reads like Taylor Maison.

The move: T directly above M, both scaled to equal visual width. Reads like a compressed version of the stacked wordmark — monogram as a shrunken echo of the full name.
Tradeoff: cleanest read at small sizes. Least "distinctive glyph" feeling because it's close to just smaller type.

The move: T and M overlap so the T's vertical stem disappears into the left-leg of the M. One fused glyph instead of two letters — how Chanel's two C's, YSL's stacked letters work. True ligature.
Tradeoff: most distinctive option. Reads slightly slower ("what letter is that?") but that hesitation is the point — it's earned recognition.

The move: TM set on a baseline with a deliberate heavy rule beneath — reads like a letterhead signature block. Editorial, publishing, literary.
Tradeoff: clearest "signature" feel. The underline is the signature move, like an auteur signing a page. Slightly horizontal — needs rectangular real estate, not a square avatar crop.

The move: T and M separated by a bold interpunct — the dot survives from the original pearl idea, but now it's punctuation inside typography, not a standalone shape. A signature rhythm.
Tradeoff: horizontal, requires width. Strongest at medium sizes; at 16px the dot can visually merge with the letters. Plays elegantly with typographic culture (manuscript · middle dots · bullet separators).

The TM ligature.

A single custom glyph. Not "T next to M" — T inside M. One crossbar makes the top of both letters. Three parallel verticals descend: the outer two become the M's legs; the center one is the T's stem, stopping short at the apex where the M's diagonals meet. Six strokes, one mark, proprietary by construction.

Why a ligature, not a monogram: A monogram is two letters sitting near each other — any studio with the initials TM could use one. A ligature is a custom letterform that only exists because Taylor Maison exists. Nobody else has this specific cut. That's what makes it a logo instead of a label.

Anatomy

How the T hides inside the M.

The six strokes
One crossbar spans the top — this is both the T's horizontal bar and the top edge of the M.

Three verticals drop from the crossbar. The outer two (left, right) descend all the way to the baseline. These are the M's legs.

The center vertical stops at mid-height — that's the T's stem, terminating at the apex where the M's inner diagonals converge.

Two diagonals travel from the top-outer corners inward and down, meeting the T's stem at its terminus. The negative space between them forms the M's inner triangles.

Read as a T, you see the crossbar and center stem. Read as an M, you see the outer verticals and inner V. Both readings are simultaneous — the eye flips between them, which is exactly what a good monogram does.

Scale test

The question: does the T read inside the M when the mark shrinks? Once the diagonals get thin enough to merge, the center stem's shortness becomes less legible — but the bold variant compensates.

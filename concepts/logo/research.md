# Logo research — decisions, traps, frameworks

*For: Taylor Maison · Date: 2026-04-18 · Scope: peer audit, monogram archetype, distinctiveness theory, master process*

---

## The short version

Four findings that should shape the next round of iteration:

1. **The peer set is not crowded — but the archetype is.** In the creative-director peer group (&Walsh, Draplin, van Schneider, Gretel, Base, Collins, Order, Manual), the dominant mark is a *wordmark*. Across ~25 peer marks sampled, **zero use a circle + monogram**. The pearl+letter direction is whitespace in this specific peer group. The risk is not saturation; the risk is what the archetype implies (seal / institutional / legacy luxury) versus what you want to signal (solo, strategic, current).

2. **The TM inside a circle has a specific failure mode the web confirmed: it reads as the ™ symbol before it reads as a name.** Every brand with TM initials that successfully solved this did it by (a) scaling the letters up dramatically, (b) pairing with color/context, or (c) making the letterforms so custom they stop being "letters" and start being a glyph. Stock Cabinet Grotesk "TM" in a circle will always read ™ first.

3. **Monograms that work do one thing: the letters *interact*.** Chanel (reflected C), Fendi (rotated F), VW (stacked inside a circle with identical stems), YSL (Cassandre drew custom letterforms that mix serif/sans in one mark). The circle is the frame; the letterform mechanic is the brand. A T and an M sitting next to each other inside a frame is two letters in a box — not a monogram.

4. **Paul Rand, Sagi Haviv, and Paula Scher would all independently push you toward type.** The peer audit agrees. The trademark law agrees. The master process agrees. This doesn't kill the pearl — but it forces the question: is the pearl the primary mark, or a secondary motif supporting a wordmark that's already strong?

## § 1 — Peer landscape

**What peer creative directors actually do for their own brands:**

Of ~25 solo creative directors, boutique studios, and design+AI consultants sampled, the distribution is:

- Pure wordmark (text-only, no symbol): **~12** — Draplin, van Schneider, Gretel, Base Design, Collins, Manual, Champions Design, Berger & Föhr, Paula Scher's personal presence, Maggie Appleton, Linus Lee, Soleio
- Ampersand or typographic glyph: **2** — &Walsh, Anton & Irene
- Personal character / person-as-brand: **2** — Draplin (orange hat icon), Ryan McGinness (methodology-as-brand)
- Symbol system (geometric, *not* contained in a circle): **3** — Order, some van Schneider client work, some Berger & Föhr projects
- **Circle + contained monogram: 0**

A few observations that matter:

**Wordmark-first is dominant because it scales economically and defers to client work.** A studio whose own mark is visually loud competes with its own clients. The restrained wordmark is a professional courtesy as much as a style choice.

**When peers do use symbols, they're geometric systems, not contained marks.** Order (Jesse Reed & Hamish Smyth, ex-Pentagram) publishes under modular type-forward systems. Not a one-shot seal.

**The circle + monogram archetype reads from law firms, luxury houses, and collegiate institutions**, not from cutting-edge creative practices. That's the real risk — not saturation, but semantic association. The mark will say "establishment" when you want it to say "current."

**Assessment:** Circle + monogram is open territory in your peer set. If you take it, you're alone in the format — which is either a breakout move or a signal that this format isn't what modern creative-director branding looks like. Probably both, depending on execution.

## § 2 — The monogram archetype: what works, what fails

**Five monograms analyzed in detail:**

- **Chanel CC (Coco Chanel, 1925).** Two Cs reflected and overlapped so their curves *share* negative space. Bold, even stems, drawn by hand. The interlock is the brand — the circle is just where the lockup lives. Remove the circle and the CC is still recognizable.
- **YSL (Cassandre, 1963).** Three letters, stacked, *with mixed serif and sans treatment* in the same mark. Custom everything. Proportions asymmetric on purpose. The mark feels like a signature because it couldn't exist in a standard typeface.
- **Fendi FF (Lagerfeld, 1965).** One F rotated 180° to make its own mirror. Economical (one letter drawn twice) but unmistakable — the negative space between the mirrored letters becomes a diamond cavity. That void is proprietary.
- **HBO (Betty Brugger, 1980).** Three bold sans letters, tightly tracked so they read as one word, with a surgical modification (the camera-lens dot in the O) that adds meaning without breaking clarity.
- **VW (1937).** V stacked on W inside a circle. What keeps it from being generic is the *proportional calibration* — identical stem weights, specific overlap geometry, and a perfect circle as the seal. Every element load-bearing.

**Common thread:** the letterforms *do something*. They reflect, rotate, interlock, share strokes, or get a specific modification. The circle is secondary to the letterform mechanic.

**Three ways generic monograms fail:**

- **Collegiate seal clones.** Two or three letters in stock Garamond inside an ornate circle. Interchangeable by design — swap the initials and nothing breaks. Zero distinctiveness.
- **Mid-market corporate letter-in-circle.** Helvetica or Arial letters inside a thin ring. No interlock, no overlap, no reason the letters are together. The circle is pure decoration. Reads as a placeholder.
- **Premature monograms.** KFC, IBM, and FedEx earned abbreviation after decades of equity. A solo creative director using a monogram from day one can read as presumptuous — "I'm already so established you'll know my initials."

**The ™ problem — confirmed and specific:**

When a brand's initials are TM, standalone use at small sizes reads as the trademark symbol. The brands that have managed it (T-Mobile being the closest example) do it by (a) making the T significantly larger than the M or surrounding it with color context, (b) custom-drawing the letterforms so they stop reading as alphabet characters, or (c) always pairing with wordmark context.

**Stock Cabinet Grotesk "TM" inside a plain circle is the worst-case version of this problem.** It's exactly the shape the brain has been trained to read as ™ for a century.

**Legal angle:** Per USPTO practice, monograms require "substantial graphic elaboration" to be protectable. Plain letters inside plain shapes get rejected for descriptiveness. Chanel, YSL, and Fendi all got protection because their letterforms were custom-drawn. A stock-type monogram has weak trademark footing from day one.

**Pitfalls checklist (keep for any monogram direction):**

- Stock typeface → generic. Letters must be customized or interlocked.
- Letters sitting isolated → reads ™. Must share a stroke, overlap, or lock.
- Circle alone does not create distinctiveness. The container is neutral; the letterform mechanic is the brand.
- Over-decoration to hide generic letters compounds the problem.
- Must survive the 16px test. If the mark collapses at favicon size, it's decoration not identity.

## § 3 — Distinctiveness framework (apply to any proposed mark)

A short diagnostic to pressure-test an option before committing:

1. **Semantic test.** Can you describe what the mark *means* (not what it looks like) in one sentence, and does that meaning connect to the business? "A pearl because I like pearls" ≠ "A pearl because the business extracts refined insight from raw brief." The first is decoration, the second is mark.

2. **Phone test.** Describe the mark in ~20 words to someone who's never seen it. Could they sketch something close? If you need qualifiers ("well, it's kind of like... but special because..."), the mark is overcomplicated.

3. **Silhouette test.** Collapse to a pure black shape at 16px. Does the form hold? Is it distinct from every other letter-in-circle mark? If it melts into the category, the form needs work.

4. **Trademark distinctiveness spectrum.** Where does the mark sit: Generic → Descriptive → Suggestive → Arbitrary → Fanciful? "Circle with T" is descriptive at best. "Pearl that implies extracted-value craftsmanship" is suggestive. Arbitrary/Fanciful is where the strongest marks live.

5. **Competitive saturation, specific.** Not "are there other circle+letter marks" (there are) but "in my category, how many?" The peer audit above says zero. That's the right cut of the saturation question.

6. **Gestalt closure.** Does negative space or figure-ground create an involuntary "aha"? Not required — but marks that have it (FedEx arrow, NBC peacock, Baskin-Robbins 31) tend to stick. Without it, the mark leans on consistency and equity to become memorable.

7. **One-color stress test.** Works in pure white on black, pure black on white, and at 16px? If it needs color or gradients to read, the core form is fragile.

8. **Memory test.** After seeing it once, can someone sketch a rough version 24 hours later? Distinctive marks have a "shape grammar" clear enough to internalize (Apple = rounded square; Dropbox = open box; NBC = peacock profile).

**Where circle-with-letter slides into generic — specific failure modes:**

- **Isolated elements.** Pearl and letter coexist but don't interact. Viewer sees "pearl brand for a T person," not one mark. *Fix:* structural overlap or closure so they feel inseparable.
- **Familiar components, familiar relationship.** Pearl is common. T is common. Pearl + T is safe, which reads as generic. *Fix:* change the *relationship*. Pearl inside the letter's negative space, or letter built from pearl-like forms.
- **No semantic link.** A pearl is beautiful — but why a pearl for a *strategy* practice? If the answer is "elegance," the mark is decoration. *Fix:* anchor the pearl to a specific Taylor Maison value. Discovered insight. Rough brief → polished strategy. Something only this business would say.

## § 4 — How the masters think

The process principles that matter most for this decision:

- **Paul Rand: "Don't try to be original; just try to be good."** Simplicity is a byproduct, not a goal. Novelty is not a virtue. The strongest marks are well-formed, not inventive.
- **Sagi Haviv's six pillars:** appropriateness, distinctiveness, simplicity, memorability, versatility, timelessness. Every serious mark must pass all six. Haviv also preaches *resisting the redesign instinct* — the NBC peacock was retained rather than reinvented because the equity was already there.
- **Paula Scher on Citibank:** "It took a few seconds to draw. It took 34 years to learn how to do that in a few seconds." Fast execution = long constraint training. Trust the judgment that rejects, not the impulse that sketches.
- **Scher on Microsoft:** "Your name is Windows. Why are you a flag?" *Name the contradiction before redesigning.* The sharpest way to kill a direction is to articulate the lie inside it.
- **Michael Bierut:** the brief must be interrogated before the form. Most logo mistakes are briefs-disguised-as-form-problems.
- **Christoph Niemann:** the concept must be singular to the business. If the mark would work for another business, it's not your mark.

**Applied critique — how Haviv and Bierut would likely read your current options:**

**Haviv (imagined, grounded in his published framework):**  
"A circle with a small TM in the upper-left and a pearl is a seal. Every professional uses a seal. It reads institutional. For a solo creative director selling strategy, I'd ask: why not a wordmark? The pearl is pleasant, but it's not distinctive enough to carry the business alone — if five solo consultants used this mark, no one would know which was which. At 16px the pearl vanishes. Kill it and come back with something that *only Taylor could own*."

**Bierut (imagined, grounded in his process):**  
"What does Taylor actually sell? Strategy. Websites. AI consulting. A pearl reads luxury and craft. That's not wrong — but is that the positioning? If strategy, then type. If craft, then maybe the pearl. You haven't finished answering the brief yet. Once you know the business in one sentence, design the mark from that sentence, not from aesthetic preference."

Neither of these is a verdict. They're the questions a senior designer would ask, and they're questions worth answering *before* committing to pearl+TM.

## § 5 — Applied to your current options

Reading the existing concept files against this research:

**Stacked wordmark (F · TAYLOR / MAISON in Cabinet Grotesk 800).**  
Matches the dominant peer archetype. Avoids every monogram pitfall. Escapes the ™ confusion entirely. Scales perfectly. Distinctive *through positioning and typography*, not through novel mark-making. Risk: plays the same game everyone else is playing — might feel unowned without a supporting glyph.

**Pearl alone (B · the original two-circle mark).**  
Google Lens flood is real at the app-icon level (music apps, wellness, lighting). In peer set: no collision. Real risk: semantically empty — pearl connotes luxury/craft but not strategy. Passes simplicity and memorability; fails the "would work for another business" test badly.

**Pearl + T (pearl-t.html, cards 1–4).**  
Best of the group: **2 · Upscaled** (bigger T, upper-left). Keeps the off-center pearl composition but locks it to your initial. The T must be *custom-drawn* to escape stock-Cabinet genericness — right now the T is geometric rects, which is the generic failure mode the research warns against. Direction is promising *if* you commit to proprietary letterform work.

**Pearl + TM / tm (pearl-t.html, cards 5–8).**  
Research strongly cautions against uppercase TM standalone. Card **6 · tm lowercase** is the one variation that escapes the ™ read and gains editorial character. The rest (5, 7, 8) carry the trademark-symbol confusion the research flagged as the specific killer of this archetype.

**Sunset pearl (pearl-sunset.html, 8 constructions).**  
Your favorite was **Graduated**. Research agrees: distinctiveness lives in the mechanic (slats, cuts, tapered slats) rather than in the container. This direction performs best on the "would work for another business" test because nobody else has exactly this mechanic. It also has semantic content — sunset, horizon, light through structure — which the plain pearl lacks. The risk is that the mark is harder to describe in one sentence ("a pearl with horizontal cuts through the lower half that thicken toward the bottom"), which fails the phone test unless the construction becomes iconic enough to earn its complexity.

## § 6 — Recommended path forward

Based on all four research threads, in order of confidence:

**1. Primary lockup is the stacked wordmark.** Matches peer pattern, defensible on every distinctiveness test, and avoids every monogram pitfall. This is the safe, correct base. Cabinet Grotesk 800 stacked TAYLOR/MAISON is already doing the job.

**2. The supporting glyph is the question.** You have three real candidates, in order of how well each survives the research:

- **Sunset pearl — Graduated construction.** Has the semantic content, the custom mechanic, and the peer-set distinctiveness. Weak spot: complexity to describe. Best bet if you want the mark to *do something*.
- **Pearl + upscaled T, custom-drawn.** Keeps the composition you liked. Works only if you commit to proprietary letterform (not geometric rects, not stock Cabinet Grotesk). Needs a real type-design pass on the T.
- **Plain pearl retained as a secondary motif, not a primary mark.** Uses it the way Chanel uses the CC — as a repeat pattern, favicon, avatar — while the wordmark does the heavy lifting. Sidesteps the genericness problem by not asking the pearl to carry the business.

**3. Kill the uppercase TM direction.** The ™ confusion is too well-documented and too consistent across the research. Lowercase `tm` survives but is a narrow path; uppercase TM inside a circle is the exact shape the brain reads as a trademark symbol.

**4. Whatever supporting glyph you pick, commit to custom letterform work.** The research is unanimous on this — the difference between Chanel's CC and any collegiate seal is that one was drawn and the other was typed. If you keep a letter in the mark, it cannot be a stock character.
